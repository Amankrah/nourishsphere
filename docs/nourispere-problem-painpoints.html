The Problem We Exist to Solve

A research-grounded articulation of the core problem, the pain points across every audience, and why the world urgently needs an integrated environmental nutrition platform.

The Core Problem

A $20 Trillion Failure of Integration

The global food system is simultaneously the leading driver of preventable disease and one of the largest contributors to environmental destruction — yet the tools, data, and decision frameworks for nutrition, health, and environmental impact remain completely siloed.

The Problem Statement

Every day, billions of food decisions are made in the dark. People choose what to eat without knowing how it will affect their health over time. Researchers study nutrition, environmental impact, and clinical outcomes in isolated silos that never converge. Policymakers craft dietary guidelines without integrated environmental cost data, and environmental policy without nutritional consequence modeling. No single system exists that unifies nutritional science, environmental life-cycle impact, and clinical health outcomes into one decision framework — leaving individuals powerless, research fragmented, and policy incomplete.
The evidence is clear: Poor-quality diets are the leading risk factor for disease globally, contributing to one in five deaths worldwide. At the same time, the food system generates roughly one-third of global greenhouse gas emissions, drives freshwater scarcity, and accelerates biodiversity loss. The cost of this dual crisis — human health damage plus environmental degradation from how we eat — approaches $20 trillion annually, more than double the direct cost of global food consumption itself. These are not two separate crises. They are one crisis, artificially divided by the boundaries of academic disciplines, government ministries, and app categories.

Pain Points by Audience

For Individuals & Families

The people making daily food decisions — the most frequent and most consequential micro-choices for both personal health and planetary impact.

🧩
Pain Point #1 — Individuals

The Fragmented Information Problem

A person who wants to eat well for their body and the planet must currently cross-reference multiple disconnected sources: a calorie tracker for macros, a separate app for environmental footprint, medical guidelines from their doctor for a chronic condition, and government dietary recommendations that don't mention environmental impact at all. The mental load is unsustainable. Most people give up and default to habit, convenience, or price — none of which optimize for health or sustainability.
People who are motivated to change their diet face cognitive overload and decision fatigue. The intention-behavior gap widens — research shows this gap is most severe for habitual behaviors like eating, where over a third of users abandon nutrition apps within months.
A single platform that shows the nutritional quality score (HSR, FCS), dietary pattern assessment (HEFI), clinical health impact (HENI in DALYs), and environmental cost (18 LCA categories, monetized) for every food and every meal — in one view, at the point of decision.
⚖️
Pain Point #2 — Individuals

The False Trade-Off Perception

Most people believe they must choose between eating healthy or eating sustainably — that plant-based equals healthy, or that environmentally friendly food is expensive and inaccessible. Without data, people operate on misconceptions. Some "healthy" foods have devastating environmental footprints. Some environmentally excellent foods are nutritionally poor. Without integrated scoring, there is no way to identify the foods that genuinely win on both dimensions.
Consumers make suboptimal choices based on incomplete mental models, either sacrificing health for perceived sustainability or ignoring environmental impact entirely because "at least it's healthy." Neither outcome is acceptable.
Side-by-side nutritional and environmental scoring per food item — with monetized environmental costs in CAD — lets users see, for the first time, which foods genuinely perform well on both axes and which involve real trade-offs.
💔
Pain Point #3 — Individuals

The Behavioral Change Failure Loop

Existing nutrition apps focus almost exclusively on calorie counting and macro tracking — a reductive approach that research shows can actually promote maladaptive behaviors, especially in young people. Less than half of nutrition apps are grounded in any behavioral theory. The result: people track obsessively for weeks, see no meaningful health outcome connection, and quit. Only 15% of users in clinical studies sustained dietary behavior change beyond 6 months. The apps fail because they measure inputs (what you ate) without connecting them to outcomes (what it's doing to your health and your planet).
A cycle of app adoption → short-term tracking → disillusionment → abandonment → guilt — repeated across millions of users, wasting motivation and eroding trust in digital health tools as a category.
By connecting every meal to validated clinical outcome indicators (HENI/DALY-based health impact) and tangible environmental costs, NouriSphère gives users outcome-level feedback, not just input tracking. Every meal becomes a measurable act with visible consequences — the foundation for sustained behavioral change.

For Researchers & Nutrition Scientists

The academics and clinical researchers working at the intersection of food science, public health, and environmental science — often unable to bridge these disciplines due to tool and data limitations.

🔬
Pain Point #4 — Researchers

The Siloed Data Infrastructure

A researcher studying the health-environment efficiency of diets must currently pull from at least four separate, unlinked databases: national nutrient composition files (like Canada's CNF), environmental LCA datasets (often paywalled or region-locked), validated nutritional scoring systems (HSR, Nutri-Score, FCS — each with different methodologies), and health outcome data (GBD, DALY estimates). Linking these requires months of data engineering before a single hypothesis can be tested. A Nature Food paper described this as a fundamental barrier to operationalizing food system monitoring.
Research that should take weeks takes years. Cross-disciplinary studies remain rare — published literature on dietary change under a consistent SDG framework is "almost non-existent." The most important questions at the nutrition-environment-health intersection go unanswered because the infrastructure to ask them doesn't exist.
5,000+ foods pre-linked across 150+ nutritional metrics, 18 environmental LCA categories, and multiple validated scoring systems (HSR, FCS, HEFI, HENI) — a unified research-ready dataset that eliminates months of data preparation and enables genuinely integrated analyses from day one.
📊
Pain Point #5 — Researchers

The Validation & Reproducibility Crisis

When researchers do manage to link nutrition and environmental data, they typically build custom, one-off analytical pipelines that are neither standardized nor reproducible. Each research group uses different LCA methodologies, different nutrient databases, different health impact models. Comparing results across studies becomes nearly impossible. The field cannot build cumulative knowledge when every team is working from a different data foundation.
Slow scientific progress, duplicated effort, and policy recommendations that rest on incomparable evidence bases. Grant funding is consumed by infrastructure-building rather than discovery.
A shared, transparent, validated analytical platform with standardized methodologies (ReCiPe 2016 midpoint LCA, Canadian regionalization, consistent scoring algorithms) — enabling reproducible research that the entire field can build upon.

For Policymakers & Public Health Organizations

The government bodies, NGOs, and international organizations responsible for dietary guidelines, food procurement standards, and the regulatory frameworks that shape what populations eat.

📋
Pain Point #6 — Policymakers

Guidelines Built on Half the Picture

National dietary guidelines in most countries are developed based on nutritional adequacy and disease prevention — without systematic consideration of environmental impact. Conversely, environmental food policies (procurement rules, carbon labeling mandates) are rarely tested against nutritional outcome data. The EU's Farm to Fork strategy, Canada's Food Guide, and WHO dietary recommendations all acknowledge this gap but lack the integrated analytical tools to close it. Policy is being written with half the evidence base.
Dietary recommendations that may inadvertently increase environmental harm, or sustainability mandates that could worsen nutritional outcomes in vulnerable populations. A 2024 analysis across 194 countries found that policy implementation scores for nutrition remain alarmingly low — and integration with environmental targets is even lower.
An evidence platform where policymakers can model dietary scenarios and instantly see the combined nutritional quality, health outcome, and environmental impact — enabling guidelines that optimize across all three dimensions rather than trading one off against another.
🌐
Pain Point #7 — Policymakers

The Cross-Border Evidence Gap

The EU and Canada are both advancing ambitious food sustainability agendas, but using fundamentally incompatible data systems and scoring models. HSR is dominant in some jurisdictions; Nutri-Score in others; FCS in research contexts. Environmental LCA data is regionalized differently. A policymaker in Brussels cannot compare the health-environment efficiency of a dietary intervention in Quebec against one in Copenhagen without extensive (and expensive) methodological harmonization.
International collaboration on food policy is stymied by data incompatibility. Best practices cannot transfer across jurisdictions. The global coordination that organizations like FAO, WHO, and the EAT-Lancet Commission call for remains aspirational rather than operational.
A platform built from day one to support multiple scoring systems (HSR, FCS, HEFI, HENI, with Nutri-Score and EU frameworks planned) and regionalized LCA data — creating a common analytical language for cross-border environmental nutrition policy.

Root Cause Analysis

Why This Problem Still Exists

Understanding why the integration gap persists is essential for positioning NouriSphère as the structural solution, not just another feature improvement.

🏛️
Root Cause #1

Disciplinary Silos in Academia & Government

Nutrition science, environmental science, and clinical medicine are separate departments with separate funding streams, separate journals, and separate career incentives. A researcher who publishes an integrated nutrition-environment-health study faces the paradox of fitting in nowhere: too environmental for nutrition journals, too nutritional for environmental journals, too theoretical for clinical ones. Government mirrors this: health ministries set dietary guidelines, environment ministries set emissions targets, and agriculture ministries set food policy — rarely in coordination.
💰
Root Cause #2

Commercial Incentives Work Against Integration

Existing nutrition apps are venture-funded consumer products optimized for engagement metrics and subscription revenue, not integrated health-environment outcomes. There is no commercial incentive to add complex LCA analysis or DALY-based health modeling — these add development cost without clear monetization. Meanwhile, academic LCA tools are designed for expert researchers, not public use. The non-profit structure of NouriSphère is not a limitation — it is the only viable entity type for building this platform, because the mission requires integration that the market has no incentive to fund.
🗄️
Root Cause #3

Data Integration Is Genuinely Hard

Linking a national nutrient composition database (5,000+ foods × 150+ nutrients) with regionalized environmental LCA data (18 impact categories × functional units) with validated health scoring systems (each with different input requirements) with clinical outcome models (DALY estimates from the Global Burden of Disease) is a non-trivial data engineering challenge that requires deep expertise in all four domains simultaneously. This rare combination of expertise is precisely what a dual-PhD McGill professor-led team brings — and why no one has built this before.
