Naming Strategy

Guiding Principles

Every name below was evaluated against these criteria, designed for a non-profit that must be taken seriously in academic circles, policy rooms, and by everyday people across languages.

🌍 Bilingual & Cross-Cultural

Names that read naturally in both English and French (a Quebec legal requirement for registered entities) and feel intuitive across EU languages — no awkward translations, no culture-specific idioms.

🔬 Academic Gravitas

Must project credibility in peer-reviewed and policy contexts. The dual-PhD McGill leadership is a differentiator — the name should feel rigorous, not like a consumer app.

🫀 Behavioral Resonance

Behavioral science shows people act on identity, not information. Names that help users see themselves as agents of change (not just data consumers) drive sustained engagement.

⚡ Digital-First Clarity

Short enough for URLs, hashtags, and app icons. Distinct in search. Ownable as a .org domain. Avoids confusion with existing health/eco brands.

"Nourishing People. Sustaining the Planet."
A fusion of Nourish (English) and Sphère (French for sphere/domain). Evokes both nutrition and the biosphere in one word. The accented "è" signals European and Francophone sophistication, while the word remains instantly legible in English. It positions the organization as governing a domain — the sphere where food, health, and planet intersect.

Strategic Recommendation

The Two-Brand Architecture

Consider a dual-identity approach that serves both your academic credibility and consumer engagement needs.

Recommended Architecture

Use NouriSphère (or Planète Santé Lab) as the organizational identity — the non-profit name on legal documents, grant applications, research papers, and policy submissions. This is the name that goes on the Quebec registry, the name that appears in journal citations, the name that EU policymakers see.

Keep EcoDish365 (or evolve it slightly) as the product/platform name — the consumer-facing digital tool. This is what individuals open on their phones, what they tell friends about, what appears in app stores.

This mirrors successful models like: the Mozilla Foundation (organization) → Firefox (product), or the Wikimedia Foundation (organization) → Wikipedia (product). It lets the non-profit name carry gravitas while the platform name carries accessibility.

For Grant Applications

"NouriSphère, a Quebec-registered non-profit advancing environmental nutrition through its EcoDish365 platform…"

For Users

"EcoDish365, powered by NouriSphère — the research behind your plate."

For EU Policy

"NouriSphère provides evidence-based tools for planetary health nutrition policy…"

Next Steps

Before You Decide

1. Domain & Trademark Search

Check .org availability for your top 3 picks. Search CIPO (Canada) and EUIPO (EU) trademark databases. Quebec's Registraire des entreprises for name reservation.

2. Linguistic Validation

Test pronunciation with native French, English, and 2-3 EU language speakers. Check for unintended meanings in German, Dutch, and Nordic languages (key EU markets).

3. Stakeholder Gut-Check

Share the top 3 names with 5-10 people from each target audience: McGill colleagues, potential EU partners, and everyday users. Watch for instant comprehension vs. confusion.

4. Visual Identity Development

Once a name is selected, develop a full visual identity system: logo, typography hierarchy, color specifications, icon set, and brand guidelines document for consistent application.
